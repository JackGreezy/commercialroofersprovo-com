Commercial Roof Inspection in Provo, UT

Roof inspections for Vineyard and Lindon commercial buildings, including moisture scanning that catches wet insulation before it costs on utility bills.

Finding Wet Insulation Before It Shows Up on the Utility Bill

An inspection that only checks the membrane surface misses the problem that actually costs building owners money over time: insulation that has quietly taken on moisture and lost most of its R-value while the roof above it still looks fine. We inspect for both the visible condition of a roof and what is happening underneath it.

The gap between what a roof looks like and what it is actually doing to a building's utility bills is bigger than most owners assume. A membrane can be watertight and still be sitting on top of insulation that lost half its thermal performance to trapped moisture years ago, and the only way to catch that difference is to check for it directly rather than reading the roof's exterior condition as a proxy for everything happening underneath.

What a Surface Walk Actually Tells You

A visual inspection catches the obvious issues: membrane blistering, seam separation, ponding water, flashing pulled away from a parapet. That is real, useful information, and every inspection we run starts there. On the new industrial and flex buildings going up around Lindon's Geneva Road corridor, surface condition is usually the whole story, since the roofs are young enough that deeper problems have not had time to develop.

On older buildings, particularly ones with a history of patch repairs, surface condition tells you less than it seems to. A membrane can look intact while trapping saturated insulation directly underneath, and that gap between what an inspection sees and what is actually happening in the assembly is where costly surprises come from.

Moisture Scanning and What It Reveals

We use moisture scanning to check insulation condition without tearing into the roof, which lets us map wet areas across a large roof surface and confirm findings with targeted core cuts rather than random sampling. On the mixed-use buildout happening in Vineyard, where new construction sits near older structures left from the former Geneva Steel site redevelopment, moisture scanning has caught insulation problems on buildings that had no visible surface symptoms at all.

Wet insulation matters for two separate reasons. It accelerates membrane failure from underneath, since trapped moisture cycling through freeze-thaw pushes upward on the membrane above it. And it quietly degrades the building's thermal performance, meaning rooftop units work harder to hold setpoint even though nothing about the roof looks wrong from the ground or from a quick surface walk.

Pre-Winter and Post-Storm Inspection Timing

We recommend inspecting before the wet spring snow season starts and again after any significant canyon-wind event, rather than relying on a single annual visit at an arbitrary time of year. A roof that was fine in September can have loosened flashing or a compromised seam by the time winter weather arrives, and catching that before the first heavy snow load matters more than catching it after.

Post-storm inspections after a documented wind or hail event serve a different purpose: establishing what changed and whether damage occurred, which matters both for maintenance planning and for any insurance documentation the owner may need later.

  • Membrane surface condition and seam integrity
  • Flashing attachment at parapets, curbs, and penetrations
  • Drain and scupper function and debris buildup
  • Moisture scan of insulation across the full roof area
  • Rooftop unit curb condition and mechanical penetration sealing
  • Edge metal and coping attachment under wind exposure

What Goes Into an Inspection Report

Every inspection produces a written report with photos, rather than a verbal summary of what we saw. Owners managing multiple properties, or property management firms overseeing buildings they do not visit often, need documentation they can act on and file, not a phone call they have to take notes during. We rank findings by urgency rather than listing everything as equally important, since a hairline sealant crack and an active leak path are not the same priority even though both technically belong on the report.

We also flag findings against what the roof's original construction documents or manufacturer warranty specified, when those records are available. A curb that was flashed correctly at installation but has since separated tells a different story than a curb that was undersized from the start, and that distinction affects whether the fix is a simple resealing job or a more involved rebuild of the detail.

Inspection Questions From Property Owners and Managers

How often should a commercial roof be inspected?

At minimum once a year, ideally before winter and again after any major wind or hail event. Roofs already showing age or prior leak history benefit from more frequent checks.

Can moisture scanning damage the roof?

No. It is a non-invasive scan across the membrane surface. We only core-cut in specific spots the scan flags, to confirm findings, not as a blanket sampling method.

What happens if wet insulation is found but the membrane looks fine?

We explain the extent of the wet area and options, which can range from a targeted repair over a small section to planning a larger recover or tear-off if the moisture is widespread.

Do you inspect roofs that are still under manufacturer warranty?

Yes, and it is worth doing, since some installation defects only become visible after a season or two of weather exposure, and catching them inside the warranty window matters.

How long does a typical inspection take?

It depends on roof size and complexity, but most single commercial buildings can be fully inspected, scanned, and documented in a single visit.
